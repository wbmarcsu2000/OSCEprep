## 🤰 Prenatal & Routine

### Aneuploidy screening options (ACOG) — `pre-aneuploidy`

**cfDNA (NIPT)**
- Offer from 10 weeks' gestation
- Detects trisomy 21, 18, 13 + sex chromosomes
- Best test for T21 (~99% detection, very low FPR)
- Screening only — does NOT detect neural tube defects
**First-trimester combined (11–13+6 wk)**
- NT ultrasound + PAPP-A + free β-hCG
- T21 pattern: ↓PAPP-A, ↑hCG, ↑NT
- T21 detection ~82–87%
**Quad screen (15–22 wk, best 16–18)**
- AFP, hCG, estriol (uE3), inhibin A
- T21: ↓AFP, ↓estriol, ↑hCG, ↑inhibin A
- T18: ↓AFP, ↓estriol, ↓hCG, normal inhibin A
- Elevated MSAFP screens for neural tube defects
**Diagnostic: CVS vs amniocentesis**
- CVS 10–13 wk; amniocentesis ≥15 wk
- Definitive karyotype/microarray (diagnostic, not a screen)
- Procedure-related loss ~0.1–0.3%
- Offer screening AND diagnostic to all, any age

*Pearls:* ACOG Practice Bulletin 226 (2020) says offer BOTH screening and diagnostic testing to every patient regardless of age — the old age-35 threshold for offering invasive testing is gone. cfDNA has the best T21 performance but is still a screen: confirm positives with CVS or amnio before any irreversible decision (false positives from confined placental mosaicism, vanishing twin, or maternal CNV/malignancy), and a low fetal fraction is itself associated with aneuploidy. cfDNA does not assess neural tube defects, so AFP on the quad screen (or a stand-alone MSAFP) still covers open NTDs.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ Procedure-related loss rate for CVS/amnio (~0.1–0.3%) — meta-analysis figures get revised; verify against current ACOG PB 226 numbers
- ⚠️ cfDNA performance/indication (currently offered to all risk levels per PB 226) — screening guidance evolves; confirm bulletin is still current

### Aspirin for preeclampsia prevention (USPSTF/ACOG) — `pre-aspirin`

**Dose & timing**
- Low-dose aspirin 81 mg PO daily
- Start between 12–28 weeks, ideally before 16 weeks
- Continue daily until delivery
**High risk — any 1 qualifies (medical)**
- Chronic hypertension
- Pregestational diabetes (type 1 or 2)
- Chronic kidney disease
- Autoimmune disease: SLE or antiphospholipid syndrome
**High risk — any 1 qualifies (obstetric)**
- Prior preeclampsia, especially with adverse outcome
- Multifetal gestation (twins or higher)
**Moderate risk — start aspirin if ≥2**
- Nulliparity
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Family history of preeclampsia (mother or sister)
- Age ≥35
- Black race/low SES, IVF, prior SGA/LBW, >10-yr pregnancy interval

*Pearls:* USPSTF names Black race and low income as moderate-risk factors explicitly as proxies for structural inequity, and many centers now offer aspirin for even a single moderate factor. Classic traps: it is low-dose 81 mg (not 325 mg), and initiation after 28 weeks is not recommended — benefit is greatest when started before 16 weeks. Aspirin cuts preeclampsia risk ~10–15% (ASPRE trial: 62% reduction in preterm preeclampsia with 150 mg) without increasing abruption or hemorrhage.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ USPSTF aspirin/preeclampsia recommendation is the 2021 statement (verified current 2026-07); check for a new USPSTF update cycle at next review
- ⚠️ Practice trend toward offering aspirin for a single moderate risk factor (esp. Black race/low income) is evolving — re-verify whether ACOG/USPSTF formalize it
- ⚠️ Some centers/FMF protocols use 150–162 mg dosing per ASPRE-style screening; US guideline dose remains 81 mg — re-verify if US guidance adopts higher dosing

### Pregnancy dating & antenatal surveillance (ACOG) — `pre-dating`

**Re-dating: US vs LMP (redate if discrepancy exceeds)**
- ≤8w6d by CRL: redate if US differs from LMP by >5 days
- 9w0d–13w6d: redate if US differs by >7 days
- Later cutoffs: 16–21w6d >10 d; 22–27w6d >14 d; ≥28w0d >21 d
- First-trimester CRL is the most accurate method to establish gestational age
**Antenatal testing: who & when**
- Start 32w0d for most at-risk; as early as 26–28w for severe high-risk cases
- Pregestational DM, cHTN, preeclampsia, FGR, oligohydramnios, prior stillbirth
- Also late-term ≥41w0d, cholestasis, alloimmunization, decreased fetal movement
- Weekly typical; twice weekly for FGR, post-term, pregestational DM
**BPP components & scoring**
- 5 parts x 2 pts each (max 10): NST + breathing, movement, tone, fluid on US
- US (30 min): breathing ≥30 s, ≥3 movements, ≥1 flex-extension, SDP ≥2 cm
- 8–10 normal; 6 equivocal (deliver if ≥37w, else repeat in 24 h); ≤4 abnormal
- Oligohydramnios needs further evaluation regardless of BPP score
- Modified BPP = NST + SDP: normal if reactive NST and pocket >2 cm
**Kick counts**
- Count-to-10: 10 movements in 2 h is reassuring; most achieve in <30 min
- <10 in 2 h or perceived decrease → same-day NST/further evaluation
- No proven stillbirth reduction; ACOG does not mandate routine daily counts

*Pearls:* BPP mnemonic: 'Test the Baby, MAN' — Tone, Breathing, Movement, Amniotic fluid, NST. Before 32 weeks a reactive NST only requires accelerations of 10 bpm x 10 sec (not 15x15). Classic trap: never re-date a well-dated pregnancy for third-trimester 'size less than dates' — that is FGR until proven otherwise.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ Antepartum surveillance details (start at 32w0d vs 26–28w, twice-weekly indications, modified BPP using SDP >2 cm rather than AFI) come from PB 229 (2021) — re-verify if ACOG reissues the antepartum fetal surveillance bulletin
- ⚠️ Kick-count stance (no mandated routine daily counts) reflects PB 229/AFFIRM-era evidence and could change with new stillbirth-prevention guidance

### GBS screening & intrapartum prophylaxis (ACOG) — `pre-gbs`

**Screening**
- Universal vaginal-rectal culture at 36 0/7–37 6/7 weeks
- A negative culture is valid for 5 weeks
**Give prophylaxis**
- GBS-positive culture this pregnancy
- GBS bacteriuria at any colony count this pregnancy (no culture needed)
- Prior infant with invasive GBS disease (no culture needed)
- Unknown status + <37 wk, ROM ≥18 h, or intrapartum fever ≥100.4°F (38°C)
- No ppx for cesarean before labor with intact membranes, even if GBS+
**First-line regimen**
- Penicillin G 5 million units IV load, then 2.5–3 million units q4h
- Alternative: ampicillin 2 g IV load, then 1 g q4h
- Adequate prophylaxis = first dose ≥4 h before delivery
**Penicillin allergy**
- Low-risk allergy: cefazolin 2 g IV load, then 1 g q8h
- High-risk (anaphylaxis): clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h if isolate susceptible
- Clinda resistance or unknown: vancomycin 20 mg/kg IV q8h (max 2 g/dose)

*Pearls:* ACOG moved screening from 35–37 to 36 0/7–37 6/7 weeks so the 5-week culture window covers delivery through 41 0/7 weeks. High-risk-allergy isolates need a D-zone test for inducible clindamycin resistance before 'susceptible' can be trusted, and erythromycin is no longer an acceptable agent. Classic traps: GBS bacteriuria is treated acutely only if ≥10^5 CFU/mL (any count still earns intrapartum ppx), and GBS+ status from a prior pregnancy is not by itself an indication this time.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ Screening window (36 0/7–37 6/7 wk) changed from 35–37 wk in the 2020 update; re-verify against latest ACOG
- ⚠️ Penicillin-allergy pathway (cefazolin vs clindamycin vs weight-based vancomycin 20 mg/kg, max 2 g/dose) reflects the 2020 revision; re-verify dosing/agents in future updates
- ⚠️ Acute-treatment bacteriuria threshold (≥10^5 CFU/mL) vs any-count intrapartum ppx is a convention worth re-confirming

### Gestational diabetes screening & diagnosis (ACOG) — `pre-gdm`

**Universal screen (24–28 wk)**
- Screen ALL pregnant patients at 24–28 weeks (two-step approach)
- Step 1: 50 g oral glucose, 1-h plasma glucose — nonfasting
- Positive screen: ≥130–140 mg/dL (institutional cutoff; 140 most specific)
**Diagnostic 100 g 3-h OGTT (Carpenter-Coustan)**
- Fasting ≥95 mg/dL
- 1 h ≥180 mg/dL
- 2 h ≥155 mg/dL
- 3 h ≥140 mg/dL
- GDM diagnosed if ≥2 values abnormal
**Glucose targets on treatment**
- Fasting <95 mg/dL
- 1-h postprandial <140 mg/dL
- 2-h postprandial <120 mg/dL
**Postpartum**
- 75 g 2-h OGTT at 4–12 weeks postpartum
- Interpret with nonpregnant (ADA) criteria for T2DM/prediabetes
- If normal, repeat diabetes screening every 1–3 years for life

*Pearls:* High-risk patients (obesity, prior GDM, A1c ≥5.7%) get early screening at the first prenatal visit, repeated at 24–28 weeks if negative. A 50 g screen ≥200 mg/dL is often treated as diagnostic without the 3-hour test. Carpenter-Coustan mnemonic: start at 95 fasting, then 180 and step down 25 then 15 (180→155→140).

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ 50 g GCT positive-screen cutoff convention (130 vs 135 vs 140 mg/dL) — institution/guideline preference may shift
- ⚠️ Diagnostic rule debate: some bodies accept ONE abnormal 3-h OGTT value; ACOG currently requires ≥2 — re-verify on ACOG updates
- ⚠️ Postpartum OGTT window (currently 4–12 weeks per ACOG/ADA) and 1–3 year rescreening interval track ADA Standards of Care, updated annually

### Infection screening in pregnancy (CDC/USPSTF) — `pre-infxn`

**Syphilis**
- Screen ALL at the first prenatal visit (RPR/VDRL or treponemal)
- Rescreen at ~28 wk AND at delivery — CDC/ACOG 2024: universal, not risk-based
- Benzathine penicillin G 2.4 MU IM is the ONLY Rx; desensitize if PCN-allergic
**HIV & hepatitis B**
- HIV: opt-out — test everyone at first visit unless the patient declines
- HIV: repeat in 3rd trimester if high risk; rapid test in labor if unknown
- HBsAg: test all at the first visit, every pregnancy
- HBsAg+ → newborn gets HBIG + hep B vaccine within 12 hours of birth
**Asymptomatic bacteriuria**
- Urine culture once at 12–16 wk or first visit (USPSTF Grade B)
- Treat if ≥100,000 CFU/mL even without symptoms
- Rx 4–7 days: nitrofurantoin, cephalexin, or fosfomycin
- Untreated → pyelonephritis, preterm birth, low birth weight
**Chlamydia & NOT routine**
- Chlamydia: screen at first visit if age <25, or ≥25 with risk factors
- Retest in 3rd trimester if <25/risk; test of cure ~4 wk after treatment
- BV: do NOT screen asymptomatic average-risk pregnancies (USPSTF D)

*Pearls:* ACOG/CDC went universal on the syphilis triple-check (first visit, ~28 wk, delivery) in 2024 amid the congenital syphilis surge — no more risk-based rescreening. Gonorrhea piggybacks on the same <25-or-risk rule as chlamydia, and hepatitis C is now a universal once-per-pregnancy screen. ASB traps: avoid nitrofurantoin/TMP-SMX in the 1st trimester when alternatives exist, and GBS bacteriuria at any colony count also buys intrapartum prophylaxis.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ Syphilis 3rd-trimester/delivery rescreen policy — ACOG universal vs CDC risk-based vs state legal mandates; evolving with congenital syphilis surge
- ⚠️ Hepatitis C universal once-per-pregnancy screening cadence (CDC/USPSTF)
- ⚠️ ASB screening interval and ≥100,000 CFU/mL threshold conventions (USPSTF)

### Nutrition, supplements & exposures (ACOG) — `pre-nutrition`

**Folic acid**
- Routine: folic acid 400 mcg daily, ≥1 month preconception through 1st trimester
- Prior NTD pregnancy: 4 mg daily, 3 months preconception through 12 weeks
**Weight gain by prepregnancy BMI (IOM)**
- BMI <18.5 (underweight): gain 28–40 lb
- BMI 18.5–24.9 (normal): gain 25–35 lb
- BMI 25–29.9 (overweight): gain 15–25 lb
- BMI ≥30 (obese): gain 11–20 lb
**Fish & food safety**
- Avoid high-mercury fish: shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish, bigeye tuna
- Low-mercury fish encouraged: 2–3 servings (8–12 oz) per week
- Listeria: no unpasteurized milk, soft cheeses, deli meats, smoked seafood, pâté
**Alcohol & caffeine**
- Alcohol: no safe amount at any point in pregnancy — abstain
- Caffeine: <200 mg/day (about one 12-oz coffee)

*Pearls:* The 4 mg dose is 10× routine — prescribe it as a separate folic acid tablet, never as extra prenatal vitamins (vitamin A teratogenicity). High-mercury fish are the big, long-lived predators; deli meats and hot dogs become acceptable if reheated until steaming hot. Twins shift weight goals up (normal BMI: 37–54 lb), and iron 27 mg/day is the other routine daily supplement.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ FDA/EPA fish advisory categories (avoid list currently: shark, swordfish, king mackerel, Gulf of Mexico tilefish, bigeye tuna, marlin, orange roughy) — list is revised periodically, re-verify examples
- ⚠️ Caffeine <200 mg/day is ACOG Committee Opinion 462 (reaffirmed); confirm it has not been updated if ACOG reissues nutrition guidance

### Rh(D) alloimmunization prevention (ACOG) — `pre-rhogam`

**Who & when**
- Rh(D)-negative and unsensitized (antibody screen negative)
- Routine antepartum dose at 28 weeks gestation
- Within 72 h of delivery of an Rh(D)-positive infant
- Also give after any potential sensitizing event
- Skip postpartum dose if newborn is Rh(D)-negative
**Dose**
- Standard 300 µg (1500 IU) IM anti-D immune globulin
- One 300 µg dose covers 30 mL fetal whole blood / 15 mL RBCs
- 50 µg microdose adequate for events before 12 weeks
**Sensitizing events**
- Miscarriage, induced abortion, or ectopic pregnancy
- CVS, amniocentesis, or cordocentesis
- External cephalic version, abruption, abdominal trauma
- Antepartum hemorrhage or 2nd/3rd-trimester fetal death
**Kleihauer-Betke dosing**
- Quantifies fetomaternal hemorrhage after large-bleed events
- Give one extra 300 µg vial per 30 mL fetal whole blood
- Vials = (mL fetal whole blood ÷ 30), round up, then add 1

*Pearls:* Give the 28-week dose even though a delivery dose usually follows — it prevents sensitization from silent third-trimester fetomaternal hemorrhage; if the 72-hour window is missed, still give up to 28 days postpartum. A positive antibody screen means the patient is already alloimmunized, so anti-D (passive immunization) no longer helps. Emerging 2024 SFP/ACOG guidance questions the need for anti-D or even Rh testing for spontaneous or induced abortion before 12 weeks, though the classic shelf answer still gives the microdose.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ First-trimester (<12 wk) anti-D/Rh-testing guidance is actively evolving (Dec 2024 ACOG Clinical Practice Update + SFP/2026 systematic reviews) — re-verify whether the microdose is still the expected shelf answer
- ⚠️ KB-based vial calculation and rounding convention vary by institution/reference — re-verify the round-and-add-1 rule
- ⚠️ 50 µg microdose vs 300 µg for pre-12-week events — confirm current ACOG dose recommendation

### Vaccines in pregnancy (ACIP/CDC) — `pre-vaccines`

**Give during pregnancy**
- Tdap at 27–36 wk EVERY pregnancy — passive neonatal pertussis immunity
- Inactivated flu vaccine (IIV/RIV) in ANY trimester during flu season
- COVID-19 vaccine safe in ANY trimester; ACOG/SMFM recommend it
**RSV protection**
- Abrysvo (RSVpreF) at 32–36 wk, seasonal only (Sep–Jan in most of US)
- Single lifetime maternal dose — do not repeat in later pregnancies
- Alternative: infant nirsevimab — maternal vaccine OR nirsevimab, not both
**Contraindicated (live)**
- MMR: live — contraindicated; avoid pregnancy 4 wk after a dose
- Varicella: live — contraindicated in pregnancy
- LAIV (nasal flu spray): contraindicated — use the inactivated shot
**Postpartum catch-up**
- Rubella nonimmune → MMR postpartum before hospital discharge
- Varicella nonimmune → dose 1 postpartum, dose 2 at 4–8 wk
- Live vaccines are safe while breastfeeding

*Pearls:* Give Tdap early in the 27–36 wk window to maximize antibody transfer, and repeat it every pregnancy even if the last dose was a year ago — a classic trap. Inadvertent MMR or varicella given in pregnancy is NOT an indication for termination. Volatile rec: in 2025 CDC pulled routine COVID-19 vaccination for healthy pregnant patients, but ACOG/SMFM still recommend it — shelf answers favor vaccination.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ RSV maternal vaccine window (32–36 wk) and seasonal timing (Sep–Jan in most of continental US) — re-verify each RSV season and for AK/S. Florida/territories
- ⚠️ COVID-19 vaccine status in pregnancy — CDC dropped routine rec in 2025 (shared clinical decision-making); ACOG/SMFM still recommend — re-verify annually
- ⚠️ Maternal RSV vaccine vs infant nirsevimab (either/or) strategy may shift with product availability and ACIP updates

### Prenatal visit schedule & labs (ACOG) — `pre-visits`

**Visit cadence**
- Every 4 weeks until 28 weeks
- Every 2 weeks from 28 to 36 weeks
- Weekly from 36 weeks until delivery
**Initial-visit labs**
- CBC, blood type & Rh, antibody screen
- HIV, syphilis, HBsAg, and hep C — all universal in pregnancy
- Rubella and varicella immunity
- Urine culture for asymptomatic bacteriuria
- GC/chlamydia if <25 yo or risk factors; Pap if due
**24–28 weeks**
- 50-g 1-hr GCT; if ≥130–140 mg/dL → 100-g 3-hr OGTT
- Repeat CBC to recheck for anemia
- Rh-negative: repeat antibody screen + anti-D immune globulin 300 mcg at 28 wk
**GBS & ultrasound**
- Anatomy ultrasound at 18–22 weeks
- GBS vaginal-rectal culture at 36 0/7–37 6/7 weeks
- GBS-positive → intrapartum IV penicillin G prophylaxis

*Pearls:* The GBS window moved from 35–37 to 36 0/7–37 6/7 weeks (2019) so the culture's ~5-week validity covers deliveries through 41 weeks; GBS bacteriuria this pregnancy or a prior GBS-affected infant earns prophylaxis with no culture needed. GCT cutoffs vary by institution (130, 135, or 140 mg/dL) — shelf questions usually use ≥140. Anti-D is repeated within 72 h of delivery if the newborn is Rh-positive and after any sensitizing event (bleeding, trauma, version); universal hep C screening each pregnancy is a 2021 addition.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ GCT screening threshold convention (130 vs 135 vs 140 mg/dL) — institutional and could shift if ACOG moves toward one-step IADPSG diagnosis
- ⚠️ GBS screening window and prophylaxis agent — currently ACOG CO 797 (2020); re-verify if a new committee opinion or AAP/ACOG joint update supersedes it
- ⚠️ Hepatitis C / viral hepatitis screening recommendations — updated 2020–2023; recheck current practice bulletin
- ⚠️ Visit cadence — ACOG tailored/telehealth prenatal schedules (MiPATH) are evolving and may change the default recommendation away from 4/2/1

## 🚨 Complications

### Antepartum hemorrhage: previa vs abruption (ACOG) — `comp-bleeding`

**Placenta previa**
- Painless bright red bleeding, classically after 20 wk; often postcoital
- NO digital exam until previa excluded — transvaginal US is safe & diagnostic
- Stable previa: scheduled cesarean at 36 0/7–37 6/7 wk
- Pelvic rest; anti-D immune globulin if Rh(D)-negative
**Placental abruption**
- Painful dark bleeding + rigid, tender uterus ± contractions/fetal distress
- RFs: HTN/preeclampsia, cocaine, abdominal trauma, smoking, prior abruption
- Dx is clinical — US misses ~50%; bleeding may be concealed retroplacental
- Complications: DIC, hypovolemic shock, fetal demise
- Unstable mother or category III tracing → emergent cesarean
**Vasa previa**
- Fetal vessels over os rupture at ROM → painless bleed + fetal bradycardia
- Blood is FETAL — small loss lethal; sinusoidal tracing = anemia
- Known vasa previa: cesarean at 34 0/7–37 0/7 wk, before labor/ROM
**Placenta accreta spectrum**
- Top RFs: prior cesarean + current previa; risk climbs with each CS
- Previa + ≥3 prior cesareans → accreta risk ≈60%
- Planned cesarean hysterectomy 34 0/7–35 6/7 wk; do NOT remove placenta

*Pearls:* Shelf shortcut is pain: painless = previa, painful = abruption; vasa previa is the only one where the blood is the baby's (Apt/Kleihauer-Betke positive). Classic trap: abruption can be fully concealed — a rigid, tender uterus with no visible bleeding is still abruption, and US cannot rule it out. A previa seen on 2nd-trimester US often 'migrates' away, so re-image at ~32 wk before booking the cesarean.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ Vasa previa delivery window (34 0/7–37 0/7 wk per ACOG CO 831 / SMFM Consult #44) — active literature debate favors 34–36 wk; re-verify if SMFM issues updated vasa previa guidance

### Shoulder dystocia (ACOG) — `comp-dystocia`

**Risk factors**
- Macrosomia: consider CD for EFW ≥5,000 g (or ≥4,500 g if diabetic)
- Diabetes (GDM or pregestational) — risk independent of birthweight
- Prior shoulder dystocia: recurrence ~10%; counsel re: elective CD
- Operative vaginal delivery (vacuum/forceps); prolonged 2nd stage
**Recognition**
- Turtle sign: head delivers, then retracts back against perineum
- Anterior shoulder impacted behind pubic symphysis; gentle traction fails
**Maneuver sequence**
- 1) McRoberts: hyperflex maternal hips onto abdomen — resolves ~40% alone
- 2) Suprapubic pressure: downward/lateral push on anterior shoulder
- 3) Deliver posterior arm: flex elbow, sweep forearm across chest
- 4) Rotational: Rubin II / Woods corkscrew to oblique diameter
- 5) Gaskin all-fours; 6) Zavanelli: replace head, emergency CD
**Complications & never-do**
- Brachial plexus palsy (Erb, C5–C6) — most transient, ~10% permanent
- Clavicle or humerus fracture — heals well without sequelae
- Hypoxic injury: risk climbs with head-to-body interval >5 min
- NEVER fundal pressure — worsens impaction; no excessive lateral traction

*Pearls:* Mnemonic HELPERR: call for Help, evaluate for Episiotomy, Legs (McRoberts), Pressure (suprapubic), Enter (rotational maneuvers), Remove posterior arm, Roll (Gaskin) — episiotomy relieves nothing bony, it only makes room for the internal maneuvers. About half of cases occur in infants <4,000 g with no risk factors, so prediction fails and every delivery team must be drill-ready. Classic trap: brachial plexus palsy can arise in utero or even at cesarean, so its presence alone does not prove excessive traction.

### Ectopic pregnancy (ACOG) — `comp-ectopic`

**Risk factors**
- Prior ectopic (strongest RF), prior tubal surgery or tubal ligation
- PID (esp. chlamydia), smoking, age >35, infertility
- IVF/ART (also heterotopic risk); pregnancy with IUD in place — think ectopic
**Diagnosis**
- No IUP on TVUS + hCG above ~3,500 (discriminatory zone) → presume ectopic
- Viable IUP: hCG rises ≥49% per 48h (start <1,500); slower rise = abnormal
- Abnormal rise/plateau = ectopic or early loss — trend alone can't localize
- hCG below zone + no IUP = pregnancy of unknown location → repeat hCG in 48h
**Methotrexate**
- Mass <3.5 cm, no fetal cardiac activity, hCG <5,000 mIU/mL
- Hemodynamically stable, unruptured, reliable follow-up guaranteed
- CI: intrauterine pregnancy, breastfeeding, renal/hepatic/heme dz, active PUD
- Single-dose MTX 50 mg/m² IM; hCG day 4 & 7 — need ≥15% fall day 4→7
**Surgery & follow-up**
- Surgery: rupture/instability, hemoperitoneum, MTX contraindicated or failed
- Laparoscopic salpingectomy or salpingostomy (tube-sparing if desired)
- Weekly hCG until undetectable after MTX or salpingostomy (may take weeks)
- Anti-D immune globulin 50 mcg if Rh(D)-negative

*Pearls:* After single-dose MTX, hCG often rises through day 4 — success is judged only by a ≥15% fall from day 4 to day 7 (<15% → repeat dose; surgery after two failures). Trap: in IVF patients a confirmed IUP does not exclude ectopic (heterotopic ~1:100 with ART). ACOG raised the discriminatory zone to ~3,500 precisely to avoid giving MTX to a desired viable IUP — never treat off a single hCG value.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ Anti-D for early ectopic: ACOG 2024 CPU (omit <12 wk, conditional) conflicts with SFP (give 50 mcg) — re-verify which stance current ACOG guidance takes
- ⚠️ Discriminatory hCG level (~3,500 mIU/mL) — confirm ACOG has not revised
- ⚠️ Minimum 48-h hCG rise tiers (49%/40%/33%) — re-verify if ACOG updates pregnancy-of-unknown-location guidance

### Hypertensive disorders: classification (ACOG) — `comp-htn-class`

**Four categories**
- Chronic HTN: BP ≥140/90 predating pregnancy or before 20 weeks
- Gestational HTN: new ≥140/90 at ≥20 wk without proteinuria or severe features
- Preeclampsia: new HTN ≥20 wk plus proteinuria or severe features
- Superimposed preE: chronic HTN with new proteinuria or severe features
**Preeclampsia diagnostic criteria**
- BP ≥140/90 on two occasions at least 4 hours apart
- Proteinuria: ≥300 mg/24 h or protein/creatinine ratio ≥0.3
- Dipstick 2+ only if quantitative methods unavailable
**Severe features (any one qualifies)**
- BP ≥160/110, confirmable within minutes
- Platelets <100,000/µL
- Creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling of baseline
- AST/ALT ≥2x normal or severe persistent RUQ/epigastric pain
- Pulmonary edema; new-onset headache or visual disturbance
**HELLP**
- Hemolysis: schistocytes, LDH ≥600 IU/L, bilirubin ≥1.2 mg/dL
- Elevated liver enzymes ≥2x normal; low platelets <100,000
- Up to 15% have no HTN or proteinuria at presentation

*Pearls:* Proteinuria is neither required (severe features alone make the diagnosis) nor a severity marker — ACOG dropped massive proteinuria (>5 g) and fetal growth restriction from the severe-features list. Gestational HTN with severe-range BPs is managed as preeclampsia with severe features. Classic trap: HELLP can present with normal BP and no proteinuria, so think of it in any third-trimester RUQ or epigastric pain.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ ACOG still defines HTN at ≥140/90 (PB 222, 2020 + interim updates); re-verify if ACOG ever adopts the ACC/AHA 130/80 thresholds under discussion
- ⚠️ ACOG issued a Clinical Practice Update on sFlt-1/PlGF biomarker prediction of preeclampsia with severe features — biomarker role may enter diagnostic criteria in future revisions

### Intra-amniotic infection & endometritis (ACOG) — `comp-infection`

**Triple I diagnosis**
- Isolated maternal fever ≥39.0°C, or 38.0–38.9°C persisting on 30-min recheck
- PLUS ≥1 of: fetal tachycardia >160 bpm for ≥10 min
- or maternal WBC >15,000/µL (in absence of corticosteroids)
- or purulent-appearing fluid from the cervical os
**Risk factors**
- Chorio: prolonged ROM ≥18 h, long labor, multiple digital exams
- Chorio: internal monitors, GBS colonization, meconium, nulliparity
- Endometritis: cesarean delivery is the #1 risk factor (esp. after labor)
**Intra-amniotic infection: management**
- Ampicillin 2 g IV q6h + gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV q24h
- If cesarean: add clindamycin 900 mg IV (or metronidazole) for anaerobes
- Deliver: augment/expedite labor — NOT an indication for immediate cesarean
- Acetaminophen for fever; alert the neonatal team
**Postpartum endometritis**
- Dx: postpartum fever + uterine (fundal) tenderness ± foul lochia
- Clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h + gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV q24h
- Add ampicillin if GBS+ or poor response (covers enterococcus)
- IV abx until afebrile and asymptomatic 24–48 h; no oral course after

*Pearls:* ACOG renamed chorioamnionitis 'intra-amniotic infection' (Triple I) after the 2015 NICHD workshop — fever alone is 'isolated maternal fever,' not chorio. Classic shelf trap: chorio is a reason to deliver, not to section — cesarean for obstetric indications only. Endometritis mnemonic: 'C after C' — Clinda + gent after Cesarean; ampicillin alone fails because anaerobes (Bacteroides) dominate.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ Empiric regimen (ampicillin + gentamicin, clinda + gent) is under active literature pressure to modernize (evidence dates to the 1980s); if ACOG replaces CO 712 with a Clinical Practice Guideline, re-verify all antibiotic choices and doses

### Early pregnancy loss (ACOG) — `comp-loss`

**Types (os + US)**
- Threatened: bleeding, CLOSED os, viable IUP with cardiac activity
- Inevitable: bleeding + OPEN os; POC not yet passed
- Incomplete: OPEN os; some POC passed, retained products on US
- Complete: CLOSED os; all POC passed, empty uterus on US
- Missed: CLOSED os, often no bleeding; retained nonviable pregnancy
**US diagnostic thresholds**
- CRL ≥7 mm with no cardiac activity = pregnancy loss
- MSD ≥25 mm with no embryo = pregnancy loss
- No embryo with heartbeat ≥14 d after sac WITHOUT yolk sac
- No embryo with heartbeat ≥11 d after sac WITH yolk sac
**Management options**
- Expectant: allow up to 8 wk; ~80% success; best for incomplete loss
- Medical: misoprostol 800 mcg vaginally, repeat once if needed
- Mifepristone 200 mg PO 24 h before miso boosts success — use if available
- Surgical: suction aspiration (OR or office MVA); ~99% success
**Who gets what**
- Stable pt: all 3 options acceptable — patient preference guides choice
- Hemorrhage, instability, or infection → surgical evacuation required

*Pearls:* Just-under-threshold findings (CRL <7 mm without cardiac activity, MSD 16–24 mm empty sac) are only 'suspicious' — repeat US in 7–10 days rather than diagnosing loss. Mife+miso comes from the 2018 PreFaiR trial (complete expulsion 84% vs 67% with miso alone) and is now ACOG-preferred when mifepristone is available. Give RhIG 50 mcg to Rh(D)-negative patients (strongest indication with surgical management) — a 2024 ACOG advisory allows forgoing it before 12 weeks, but the shelf still expects it.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ RhIG before 12 weeks: 2024 ACOG Clinical Practice Update suggests forgoing routine Rh testing/RhIG for loss or abortion <12 0/7 wk — actively evolving area with shelf-exam lag; re-verify against latest ACOG guidance and question-bank convention
- ⚠️ Mifepristone access/REMS status affects the 'use if available' framing of mife+miso; re-verify regulatory status

### Postpartum hemorrhage (ACOG) — `comp-pph`

**Definition**
- Cumulative blood loss ≥1000 mL within 24 h of delivery, regardless of route
- OR any blood loss with signs/symptoms of hypovolemia
**Etiology — the 4 Ts**
- Tone: uterine atony — most common cause (~70–80%)
- Trauma: lacerations, hematoma, uterine rupture or inversion
- Tissue: retained placenta or clots; placenta accreta spectrum
- Thrombin: coagulopathy (DIC, abruption, HELLP, anticoagulation)
**Stepwise medical management**
- First: bimanual uterine massage + oxytocin IV infusion
- Methylergonovine 0.2 mg IM — contraindicated in hypertension
- Carboprost (PGF2α) 250 mcg IM — contraindicated in asthma
- Misoprostol 800–1000 mcg sublingual or rectal
- TXA 1 g IV, ideally within 3 hours of delivery
**Refractory / procedural**
- Intrauterine balloon tamponade (Bakri) or vacuum device
- Surgical: B-Lynch compression suture, uterine artery ligation
- Hysterectomy — definitive last resort

*Pearls:* The uterotonic contraindications are shelf gold: 'methHERgine — check HER blood pressure' (avoid in hypertension/preeclampsia) and carboprost in asthmatics. The WOMAN trial showed TXA's mortality benefit only when given within 3 hours of birth. Classic trap: healthy parturients compensate until >1500 mL lost — tachycardia may be the only early sign, so escalate before hypotension appears.

### PROM & PPROM (ACOG) — `comp-prom`

**Diagnosis**
- Sterile speculum exam: pooling of fluid in the posterior fornix
- Ferning (arborization) of dried fluid on microscopy
- Nitrazine turns blue: amniotic pH 7.1–7.3 vs normal vaginal 3.8–4.5
- NO digital exams until active labor — shortens latency, raises infection risk
**Term PROM (≥37 0/7 wk)**
- Induce labor (oxytocin) rather than expectant management
- GBS prophylaxis per usual screening/risk criteria
**PPROM by gestational age**
- ≥34 0/7 wk: proceed to delivery
- 24 0/7–33 6/7 wk: expectant management with inpatient monitoring
- Antenatal steroids: betamethasone 12 mg IM q24h ×2 doses
- Latency abx: IV ampicillin ×48 h → PO amoxicillin ×5 d (7 d total)
- Plus azithromycin 1 g PO once (replaces older erythromycin)
**Deliver now at any GA**
- Intraamniotic infection (chorioamnionitis) — deliver + broad-spectrum abx
- Abruption, nonreassuring fetal status, or cord prolapse
- Magnesium sulfate for neuroprotection if <32 wk and delivery imminent

*Pearls:* Nitrazine false positives: blood, semen, BV, and alkaline urine — pooling plus ferning is more specific. The 2020 ACOG update permits shared decision-making (expectant management to 37 0/7 wk) at 34 0/7–36 6/7 wk, but the shelf answer remains deliver at ≥34. Mnemonic for deliver-now triggers: CAN — Chorioamnionitis, Abruption, Nonreassuring tracing.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ Latency-antibiotic macrolide choice: azithromycin vs erythromycin — meta-analyses and an ongoing RCT (NCT06273891) may lead ACOG to formally adopt azithromycin; re-verify wording at next PB 217 revision.
- ⚠️ 34 0/7–36 6/7 wk PPROM disposition: current PB 217 allows shared decision-making (expectant to 37 0/7) vs delivery at >=34; a future revision could shift the default answer.
- ⚠️ Lower GA bound for expectant management (24 0/7): periviable PROM counseling evolving — see ACOG May 2025 Practice Advisory on previable/periviable PROM maternal morbidity.

### Preterm labor (ACOG/SMFM) — `comp-ptl`

**Steroids & magnesium**
- Betamethasone 12 mg IM ×2, 24 h apart, for 24 0/7–33 6/7 wk at risk within 7 d
- Late preterm 34 0/7–36 6/7 wk: one course if no prior steroids, delivery ≤7 d
- Rescue: one repeat course if <34 wk, prior course ≥14 d ago, delivery risk <7 d
- MgSO₄ for neuroprotection <32 wk when delivery expected <24 h (↓cerebral palsy)
**Tocolysis (<34 wk)**
- Indomethacin first-line <32 wk; avoid ≥32 wk (ductus closure, oligohydramnios)
- Nifedipine first-line 32–34 wk; avoid with hypotension/preload-dependent lesions
- Course ≤48 h — buys time for steroids, magnesium, and transfer to tertiary care
- No maintenance tocolysis after the acute episode; do not tocolyze ≥34 wk
**Tocolysis contraindications**
- Fetal: intrauterine demise, lethal anomaly, nonreassuring fetal status
- Maternal: severe preeclampsia, hemodynamic instability, chorioamnionitis
- Also: heavy bleeding/abruption; PPROM is a relative contraindication
**fFN & cervical-length triage**
- TVUS CL >30 mm: preterm birth unlikely — no admission or treatment
- CL <20 mm: high risk — manage as preterm labor (steroids ± tocolysis)
- CL 20–30 mm indeterminate → fFN; negative fFN NPV ~99% for birth within 14 d
- fFN unreliable after intercourse, bleeding, or digital exam in prior 24 h

*Pearls:* Tocolysis never stops preterm birth — it buys 48 h for steroids, magnesium, and transfer. The ALPS trial drove late-preterm betamethasone (watch neonatal hypoglycemia; skip it with chorioamnionitis or prior steroids). Classic traps: indomethacin ≥32 wk closes the ductus, nifedipine plus magnesium risks hypotension, and prolonged terbutaline carries an FDA boxed warning.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ fFN triage role: ACOG has been de-emphasizing fFN (poor PPV; assay availability shrinking in the US) — re-verify the CL 20-30 mm → fFN algorithm still reflects practice
- ⚠️ Periviable steroid lower bound (22 0/7 vs 23 0/7 wk) is actively evolving — re-check current ACOG/SMFM periviability guidance
- ⚠️ MgSO4 neuroprotection upper limit: most use <32 wk but some protocols/trials extend toward 33-34 wk — re-verify if ACOG updates

### Severe preeclampsia & eclampsia (ACOG) — `comp-severe-pre`

**Magnesium sulfate**
- MgSO4: load 4–6 g IV over 20–30 min, then 1–2 g/h maintenance infusion
- Therapeutic Mg 4.8–8.4 mg/dL; monitor DTRs, RR, urine output
- Toxicity: DTRs lost 9–12 → resp depression 12–18 → arrest 24–30 mg/dL
- Antidote: stop drip, calcium gluconate 1 g IV over 5–10 min
**Acute severe HTN**
- Treat sustained SBP ≥160 or DBP ≥110 within 30–60 min
- IV labetalol 20→40→80 mg q10min; avoid in asthma, HF, bradycardia
- IV hydralazine 5–10 mg q20min; watch for maternal hypotension
- PO nifedipine IR 10–20 mg, repeat in 20 min; first-line if no IV
**Delivery timing**
- PreE without severe features: deliver at 37 0/7 weeks
- Severe features: deliver at 34 0/7 wk; expectant <34 only if stable
- Any GA: deliver for eclampsia, DIC, abruption, pulm edema, fetal distress
- Betamethasone <34 wk, but never delay delivery when unstable
**Eclampsia response**
- Seizure: ABCs — lateral position, O2, prevent injury; most self-limit
- MgSO4 4–6 g IV load; recurrent seizure on mag → extra 2 g bolus
- Deliver after maternal stabilization; seizure alone ≠ stat cesarean

*Pearls:* Magnesium prevents the next seizure but does not lower BP — always pair it with an antihypertensive, and remember it is renally cleared, so oliguria or renal impairment is the classic setup for toxicity. Up to a third of eclamptic seizures occur postpartum, and eclampsia can strike without preceding proteinuria or severe-range BP. Benzodiazepines and phenytoin are inferior to magnesium for eclampsia (Collaborative Eclampsia and Magpie trials).

## 👶 Labor & Monitoring

### Labor protraction & arrest (ACOG) — `labor-arrest`

**Active-phase arrest (≥6 cm)**
- Active phase begins at 6 cm; slow progress before 6 cm is not arrest
- Arrest: ≥6 cm + ROM + no cervical change ×4 h despite adequate contractions
- OR no change ×6 h of oxytocin if contractions inadequate
- Adequate contractions = ≥200 Montevideo units (needs IUPC)
**Second-stage arrest**
- Nullip: ≥3 h pushing without descent or rotation (≥4 h if epidural)
- Multip: ≥2 h pushing without descent or rotation (≥3 h if epidural)
**Protraction & failed IOL**
- Protraction = slower-than-normal progress; arrest = no progress
- Protraction alone is never an indication for cesarean
- Prolonged latent phase (>20 h nullip, >14 h multip) ≠ CD indication
- Failed IOL: no ctx + no cervical change after ≥24 h oxytocin with ROM
**Responses**
- Protraction: oxytocin augmentation ± amniotomy
- Confirmed active-phase arrest → cesarean delivery
- Second-stage arrest: operative vaginal delivery or manual rotation of OP

*Pearls:* The 2014 ACOG/SMFM Obstetric Care Consensus moved active-phase onset from 4 to 6 cm (Zhang curve replacing Friedman) — most historic 'arrest' cesareans were actually mislabeled latent phase. Second-stage mnemonic: 3-2-1 (nullip 3 h, multip 2 h, add 1 h for epidural), and the clock only counts with zero descent AND zero rotation. Classic shelf trap: diagnosing arrest at 5 cm or with intact membranes — both violate the definition.

### Cesarean indications & delivery timing (ACOG) — `labor-cesarean`

**Indications & arrest criteria**
- Top indications: arrest of labor, nonreassuring FHT, malpresentation, previa
- 1st-stage arrest: ≥6 cm + ROM, no change ≥4 h adequate ctx or ≥6 h on oxytocin
- 2nd-stage arrest: ≥3 h pushing nullip, ≥2 h multip (+1 h with epidural)
- Category III tracing unresponsive to resuscitation → expedited delivery
**Timing of scheduled delivery**
- Elective (no medical indication): not before 39 0/7 wk
- Placenta previa (uncomplicated): 36 0/7–37 6/7 wk
- Placenta accreta spectrum: 34 0/7–35 6/7 wk
- Prior classical cesarean: 36 0/7–37 0/7 wk
**Infection prevention**
- Cefazolin 2 g IV ≤60 min before incision (3 g if ≥120 kg)
- Add azithromycin 500 mg IV if in labor or membranes ruptured
- Chlorhexidine–alcohol abdominal prep; povidone-iodine vaginal prep if laboring
**ERAS essentials**
- Multimodal analgesia: scheduled acetaminophen + NSAIDs, spare opioids
- Early oral intake, early ambulation, Foley removal by ~12 h
- Pneumatic compression devices on before incision for VTE prophylaxis
- Delayed cord clamping 30–60 s; skin-to-skin in the OR when stable

*Pearls:* The shelf trap is applying the 39-week rule to everything — previa, accreta, and prior classical scars are deliberately delivered early-term/late-preterm, with no amniocentesis for lung maturity needed. Azithromycin adjunct comes from the C/SOAP trial and applies only to intrapartum/ruptured-membrane cesareans. A prior low-transverse scar is a VBAC candidate; a classical (vertical fundal) scar mandates repeat cesarean at 36–37 wk before labor onset (rupture risk ~5–10%).

### FHR decelerations (ACOG) — `labor-decels`

**Early**
- Gradual (onset to nadir ≥30 s); nadir mirrors the contraction peak
- Cause: fetal head compression (vagal) — benign, no intervention needed
**Late**
- Gradual; onset, nadir, and recovery all lag the contraction start, peak, end
- Cause: uteroplacental insufficiency
- Rx: lateral position, O2, IV fluid bolus, stop oxytocin, correct hypotension
**Variable**
- Abrupt (onset to nadir <30 s); drop ≥15 bpm lasting ≥15 s but <2 min
- Cause: umbilical cord compression
- Rx: reposition mother; amnioinfusion for recurrent variables
**Recurrent & prolonged**
- Recurrent = with ≥50% of contractions in a 20-min window
- Intermittent = with <50% of contractions
- Prolonged = drop ≥15 bpm lasting ≥2 min but <10 min
- ≥10 min = baseline change, not a deceleration
- Prolonged Rx: exam for cord prolapse; terbutaline 0.25 mg SC if tachysystole

*Pearls:* VEAL CHOP: Variable–Cord, Early–Head, Accel–OK, Late–Placental insufficiency. The 30-second onset-to-nadir cutoff (gradual vs abrupt) — not depth — is what separates the types. Recurrent lates or variables WITH absent baseline variability (or bradycardia) = Category III: resuscitate and expedite delivery if no rapid recovery.

### Intrapartum emergencies (ACOG) — `labor-emergencies`

**Cord prolapse**
- Elevate presenting part off the cord with a vaginal hand until delivery
- Knee-chest or steep Trendelenburg to relieve cord compression
- Emergent cesarean unless vaginal delivery is imminent
**Uterine rupture**
- First sign: sudden fetal bradycardia or recurrent decelerations
- Constant abdominal pain + loss of fetal station + vaginal bleeding
- Risk: prior uterine scar — TOLAC rupture ~0.5–1% (low transverse)
- Immediate laparotomy: deliver fetus, repair uterus or hysterectomy
**Amniotic fluid embolism**
- Triad: sudden hypoxia + hypotension/CV collapse + DIC
- Timing: labor or within 30 min postpartum; diagnosis of exclusion
- Supportive: intubate, vasopressors, blood products/massive transfusion
- Maternal arrest: CPR with perimortem cesarean by 4–5 min
**Uterine inversion**
- Shock + hemorrhage + no palpable fundus right after delivery
- Do NOT remove the placenta until the uterus is replaced
- Replace immediately (Johnson maneuver); nitroglycerin or terbutaline to relax
- Once replaced: oxytocin to contract the uterus and prevent re-inversion

*Pearls:* Classic traps: never handle or replace a prolapsed cord (vasospasm), and never pull the placenta off an inverted uterus — it worsens hemorrhage. Temporize cord prolapse en route to the OR with bladder filling (~500–700 mL) or terbutaline 0.25 mg SC. Rupture risk with a prior classical incision is ~4–9% (why classical scar contraindicates TOLAC), and think AFE over PE when collapse comes with DIC.

### FHR interpretation & categories (ACOG) — `labor-fhr`

**Baseline**
- Normal baseline 110–160 bpm; bradycardia <110, tachycardia >160
- Baseline = mean FHR over 10 min window, requires ≥2 min of identifiable baseline
**Variability & accelerations**
- Absent = undetectable; minimal = detectable but ≤5 bpm
- Moderate = 6–25 bpm; marked = >25 bpm
- Acceleration = ≥15 bpm above baseline lasting ≥15 sec (≥32 weeks)
- <32 weeks: ≥10 bpm lasting ≥10 sec qualifies as an acceleration
**Category I (needs ALL)**
- Baseline 110–160 bpm
- Moderate variability
- No late or variable decelerations
- Early decels and accels may be present or absent
**Categories III & II**
- Cat III: absent variability + recurrent lates, recurrent variables, or brady
- Cat III: sinusoidal pattern alone qualifies
- Category II = everything not Category I or III (indeterminate)

*Pearls:* Category II is the wastebasket holding most intrapartum tracings — minimal variability, or recurrent variables WITH moderate variability, are classic Category II traps. An acceleration lasting ≥2 min is 'prolonged'; ≥10 min is a baseline change. Sinusoidal pattern means severe fetal anemia until proven otherwise (alloimmunization, fetomaternal hemorrhage, ruptured vasa previa).

### Induction of labor (ACOG) — `labor-induction`

**Indications (with timing)**
- Late-term: offer induction at 41 0/7 wk; induce by 42 0/7 wk
- Preeclampsia: deliver at 37 wk; with severe features at 34 wk
- Pregestational DM or GDM on meds: deliver at 39 wk
- FGR/IUGR: deliver 37–39 wk; earlier if abnormal Doppler
- Isolated oligohydramnios: deliver 36 0/7–37 6/7 wk
**Bishop score**
- Components: dilation, effacement, station, consistency, position
- Bishop ≥8: favorable — success rate ≈ spontaneous labor
- Bishop ≤6: unfavorable — ripen cervix before oxytocin
**Ripening & induction agents**
- Misoprostol (PGE1) 25 mcg vaginally q3–6h; NEVER with prior cesarean
- Dinoprostone (PGE2) gel/insert; remove insert if tachysystole
- Foley balloon: mechanical; the ripening choice after prior cesarean
- Oxytocin IV titrated; target ctx q2–3 min (~200 Montevideo units)
**Contraindications**
- Placenta previa or vasa previa
- Prior classical incision or transfundal uterine surgery
- Transverse lie; cord prolapse; active genital HSV outbreak

*Pearls:* ARRIVE (2018): elective induction at 39 wk in low-risk nulliparas is reasonable and modestly lowers cesarean rates. Tachysystole = >5 contractions per 10 min averaged over 30 min — remove the agent and consider terbutaline. Classic trap: a prior LOW transverse cesarean is NOT a contraindication to induction itself — only prostaglandins are off-limits; Foley plus oxytocin is fine.

### Operative vaginal delivery (ACOG) — `labor-operative`

**Indications**
- Prolonged 2nd stage: nullip ≥3 h (+1 h if epidural); multip ≥2 h (+1 h)
- Nonreassuring fetal heart tracing (suspected fetal compromise)
- Maternal exhaustion or inadequate expulsive effort
- Shorten pushing for maternal disease (cardiac, cerebrovascular)
**Prerequisites**
- Cervix fully dilated (10 cm) and membranes ruptured
- Head engaged (≥0 station); fetal position known with certainty
- Bladder empty (drain with catheter)
- No suspected cephalopelvic disproportion; EFW assessed
- Adequate anesthesia, consent, willingness to abandon for cesarean
**Vacuum risks**
- Cephalohematoma: subperiosteal blood, does NOT cross suture lines
- Subgaleal hemorrhage: crosses sutures; can be life-threatening
- Also retinal hemorrhage, scalp laceration, neonatal jaundice
- No vacuum <34 wk; stop after 3 pop-offs or no descent with 3 pulls
**Forceps risks**
- Facial nerve (CN VII) palsy — usually resolves spontaneously
- More 3rd/4th-degree perineal lacerations than vacuum
- Facial marks/lacerations; rare skull fracture or ocular injury

*Pearls:* The trade-off to keep straight: vacuum spares mom (fewer severe lacerations) but risks the fetal scalp; forceps spare the scalp but tear mom. Subgaleal blood tracks between galea and periosteum, so a boggy swelling crossing sutures with rising heart rate is hemorrhagic shock brewing — unlike the benign, suture-limited cephalohematoma. Never rescue a failed vacuum with forceps: sequential instruments compound neonatal injury, so a failed attempt goes to cesarean.

### Intrauterine resuscitation (ACOG) — `labor-resusc`

**First-line maneuvers**
- Reposition to left or right lateral decubitus (relieves aortocaval compression)
- Oxygen 10 L/min via nonrebreather face mask
- IV fluid bolus: 500-1000 mL lactated Ringer's
- Discontinue oxytocin; remove cervical ripening agent if in place
- Second stage: slow or pause pushing (e.g., push every other contraction)
**Tachysystole**
- Definition: >5 contractions per 10 min, averaged over a 30-min window
- Applies in spontaneous or induced labor, with or without FHR changes
- Terbutaline 0.25 mg SC if tachysystole persists after oxytocin stopped
**Targeted interventions**
- Recurrent lates → uteroplacental cause; recurrent variables → cord compression
- Amnioinfusion (saline via IUPC) for recurrent variable decelerations
- Scalp stimulation: elicited accel (15 bpm x 15 s) predicts pH ≥7.20
- Exam to exclude cord prolapse or rapid descent with abrupt bradycardia
**Escalation**
- Unresolved Category III despite maneuvers → move to expedited delivery

*Pearls:* Bundle mnemonic: turn the mother, turn up the fluids, turn off the pit, turn on the O2. Recent RCT data suggest maternal O2 adds little in normoxemic women, but it remains the classic shelf answer. Traps: amnioinfusion requires ruptured membranes plus an IUPC, and scalp stim is done between decels — an elicited accel rules out acidemia, but its absence does not confirm it.

### Stages & phases of labor (ACOG) — `labor-stages`

**Stage & phase definitions**
- Stage 1: labor onset to complete cervical dilation (10 cm)
- Latent phase: labor onset to 6 cm; active phase begins at 6 cm (not 4 cm)
- Stage 2: complete dilation to delivery of the fetus
- Stage 3: delivery of the fetus to delivery of the placenta
**Normal durations**
- Prolonged latent phase: >20 h nullip, >14 h multip (Friedman)
- Active phase may progress ~0.5-0.7 cm/h; old 1.2 cm/h rule is outdated
- Stage 2 normal: nullip ≤3 h (≤4 h with epidural)
- Stage 2 normal: multip ≤2 h (≤3 h with epidural)
- Stage 3 normally ~5-6 min; prolonged if ≥30 min (any parity)
**Latent-phase management**
- Prolonged latent phase alone is NOT an indication for cesarean
- Delay admission until active labor in uncomplicated patients
- Options: expectant management or therapeutic rest (e.g., IM morphine)
- Most patients with prolonged latent phase enter active labor spontaneously

*Pearls:* The 6-cm active-labor threshold comes from the 2014 ACOG/SMFM Obstetric Care Consensus (Zhang labor curves), replacing Friedman's 4 cm — the classic trap is diagnosing active-phase arrest before 6 cm, which is never valid. True arrest requires ≥6 cm with ruptured membranes plus no cervical change for ≥4 h of adequate contractions (or ≥6 h of inadequate contractions despite oxytocin). Beyond 30 min in stage 3, think retained placenta: prepare for manual extraction and watch for postpartum hemorrhage.

⚠️ **Volatile — re-verify on guideline updates:**
- ⚠️ Second-stage duration convention: ACOG CPG No. 8 (2024) uses pushing-time thresholds without an epidural add-on, but NBME/shelf materials may still test the 2014 '+1 h with epidural' rule — re-verify which convention the target exam uses and whether ACOG has issued a newer CPG.
- ⚠️ Active-phase arrest criteria wording (4 h adequate vs 6 h inadequate uterine activity with oxytocin) — re-verify against the latest ACOG CPG/consensus revision, as labor-management guidance was substantially revised in 2024 and may be updated again.

### TOLAC / VBAC (ACOG) — `labor-tolac`

**Candidates & success predictors**
- Candidates: 1 or 2 prior low-transverse cesarean deliveries
- Overall VBAC success rate ~60-80%
- Strongest success predictor: prior vaginal delivery, especially prior VBAC
- Lower success: recurrent indication (arrest of labor), obesity, induction
**Contraindications**
- Prior classical or T-shaped uterine incision (rupture ~4-9%)
- Prior uterine rupture
- Placenta previa or other contraindication to vaginal delivery
- Prior extensive transfundal surgery (cavity-entering myomectomy)
**Rupture risk & logistics**
- Uterine rupture risk ~0.5-1% with one prior low-transverse CS
- Avoid misoprostol for ripening with prior CS; oxytocin acceptable
- TOLAC only where staff for emergency cesarean immediately available
**Rupture signs**
- Most common sign: abrupt FHR abnormality, esp. fetal bradycardia
- Loss of fetal station (presenting part recedes)
- Constant abdominal pain persisting between contractions
- Vaginal bleeding; maternal tachycardia or hypotension

*Pearls:* On the shelf, sudden fetal bradycardia in a laboring TOLAC patient is rupture until proven otherwise — the answer is immediate laparotomy/cesarean, not intrauterine resuscitation. Epidurals are NOT contraindicated in TOLAC and rarely mask rupture pain. Two prior low-transverse cesareans still qualify per ACOG, with only a modest bump in rupture risk (~0.9-1.8%).

## 🌸 GYN

### Abnormal cervical screening management (ASCCP) — `gyn-asccp`

**ASC-US triage**
- Reflex HPV testing is the preferred triage for ASC-US
- ASC-US + HPV-negative → return to routine screening (repeat in 3 years)
- ASC-US + HPV-positive → colposcopy
- If HPV testing unavailable: repeat cytology in 1 year
**Colposcopy triggers**
- LSIL in patients ≥25 → colposcopy
- ASC-H or HSIL at any age → colposcopy; never just repeat the Pap
- AGC (all subtypes) → colposcopy + endocervical curettage
- AGC: add endometrial biopsy if ≥35 or risk (AUB, obesity, anovulation)
**Histology → treatment**
- CIN1: observation only — no excision for low-grade histology
- CIN2/3 in patients ≥25 → excision (LEEP or cold-knife cone)
- HSIL cytology age ≥25: expedited excision without biopsy is acceptable
**Ages 21–24: conservative**
- ASC-US or LSIL: repeat cytology at 12 months, not colposcopy or HPV
- Colposcopy only for ASC-H, HSIL, AGC, or persistence at 24 months
- CIN2 + fertility concerns: observe with colpo + cytology q6mo

*Pearls:* The 2019 update is risk-based — 'manage the risk, not the Pap': colposcopy at ≥4% immediate CIN3+ risk, expedited excision acceptable at ≥60% (e.g., HPV16-positive HSIL). Classic trap: AGC is never triaged with repeat cytology or HPV alone, because glandular disease can skip lesions — hence ECC for all plus endometrial sampling when ≥35 or at risk. In pregnancy, colposcopy is safe but ECC and endometrial biopsy are contraindicated, and excision for CIN2/3 is deferred until postpartum.

### Abnormal uterine bleeding workup (ACOG) — `gyn-aub`

**Etiologies (PALM-COEIN)**
- PALM (structural): Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy/hyperplasia
- COEIN: Coagulopathy, Ovulatory, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, Not yet classified
**Initial workup**
- hCG first in every patient — exclude pregnancy before anything else
- CBC and TSH in all patients
- Heavy menses since menarche (adolescent): coags + vWF panel to r/o vWD
- TVUS is first-line imaging to find structural (PALM) lesions
**Endometrial biopsy (EMB) indications**
- EMB in all women ≥45 with AUB
- EMB if <45 + unopposed estrogen (obesity, PCOS, chronic anovulation)
- EMB if <45 with persistent AUB or failed medical therapy
- EMB at any age if Lynch syndrome (40–60% lifetime endometrial CA risk)
**Acute severe bleeding**
- IV conjugated equine estrogen 25 mg q4–6h
- Monophasic OCP (35 mcg EE) TID x 7 days
- TXA 1.3 g PO TID or 10 mg/kg IV q8h
- Alternative: medroxyprogesterone 20 mg PO TID x 7 days

*Pearls:* Classic trap: no lab, ultrasound, or biopsy matters until hCG excludes pregnancy. Up to 20% of adolescents hospitalized for heavy menses have a bleeding disorder — vWD is the most common. Avoid IV estrogen with VTE history or active thromboembolism; reach for TXA or high-dose progestin instead (and note TXA + estrogen together raises thrombosis concern).

### Contraception selection & contraindications (CDC) — `gyn-contraception`

**Estrogen contraindications (US MEC 4)**
- Migraine WITH aura — no estrogen at any age
- Age ≥35 AND smoking ≥15 cigarettes/day
- Personal history of VTE (DVT or PE)
- Uncontrolled HTN: SBP ≥160 or DBP ≥100
- Current breast cancer — bars ALL hormonal methods
**Postpartum & alternatives**
- <21 days postpartum: no estrogen (peak VTE risk)
- Estrogen contraindicated → progestin-only pill, implant, DMPA, or IUD
**Effectiveness tiers**
- Top tier = LARC: implant + IUDs, <1% failure, not user-dependent
- Etonogestrel implant is the single most effective method (~0.05%)
- Middle tier: DMPA, pills, patch, ring (~4–7% typical-use failure)
- Bottom tier: condoms, withdrawal, fertility awareness (≥13%)
**Method-specific caveats**
- DMPA: reversible bone density loss (boxed warning) + weight gain
- IUDs are safe in nulliparous women and adolescents
- Copper IUD: hormone-free; most effective EC within 5 days

*Pearls:* Migraine WITHOUT aura is not absolute (MEC 2 if <35) — the aura is what matters; likewise age ≥35 smoking <15/day is only MEC 3. Current breast cancer bars all hormonal methods, making copper IUD the exam answer. DMPA bone loss reverses after stopping, so no DXA monitoring is needed.

### Emergency contraception (ACOG) — `gyn-ec`

**Methods & time windows**
- Copper IUD: most effective EC (>99%); insert up to 5 days after sex
- Ulipristal 30 mg PO once (ella): effective up to 120 h (5 days); Rx only
- LNG 1.5 mg PO once (Plan B): best within 72 h; OTC for all ages
**Efficacy & mechanism**
- Efficacy ranking: copper IUD > ulipristal > LNG
- Oral EC works by delaying ovulation; no method disrupts an implanted pregnancy
- Ulipristal delays ovulation even after the LH surge starts; LNG cannot
- LNG efficacy falls over 72 h; ulipristal holds steady through 120 h
**Obesity & caveats**
- Obesity lowers LNG efficacy (BMI >=26); ulipristal works up to BMI ~35
- Copper IUD efficacy unaffected by BMI — best EC choice in obesity
- Repeat the dose if vomiting within 3 h of oral EC
**After taking EC**
- Wait 5 days after ulipristal before starting hormonal contraception
- After LNG EC, hormonal contraception may start or resume immediately
- Use barrier/abstinence for 7 days after (re)starting the hormonal method
- Urine pregnancy test if no menses within 3 weeks of EC

*Pearls:* The 5-day wait exists because ulipristal is a progesterone-receptor modulator — starting a progestin right away blunts its EC effect (and vice versa). Newer data show the LNG 52 mg IUD is noninferior to copper for EC, and ACOG now lists it as an option. Classic trap: no pregnancy test, exam, or BMI cutoff is required before providing EC — advance provision is encouraged.

### Menopause & hormone therapy (ACOG/NAMS) — `gyn-menopause`

**Diagnosis**
- Clinical dx: 12 months of amenorrhea; median age ~51
- FSH not required for dx; levels fluctuate in perimenopause
**HT candidacy & regimen**
- Systemic HT = most effective tx for vasomotor symptoms
- Offer if age <60 OR within 10 years of menopause onset
- Uterus intact → estrogen + progestin
- Unopposed estrogen with a uterus → endometrial hyperplasia/cancer
- No uterus (post-hysterectomy) → estrogen alone
**Contraindications**
- History of breast cancer or estrogen-dependent tumor
- CAD, prior VTE, stroke, or TIA
- Undiagnosed abnormal vaginal bleeding
- Active liver disease
**Nonhormonal & GSM**
- SSRIs/SNRIs: venlafaxine; paroxetine (only FDA-approved SSRI)
- Gabapentin, especially for nighttime hot flashes
- GSM (dryness/dyspareunia) → low-dose vaginal estrogen
- Vaginal estrogen: minimal systemic absorption, no progestin needed

*Pearls:* Classic trap: WHI risks came from women starting HT years past menopause — the 'timing hypothesis' is why the <60/<10-year window exists. Fezolinetant (NK3-receptor antagonist, 2023) is a newer nonhormonal vasomotor option. Low-dose vaginal estrogen for GSM may even be considered in breast cancer survivors after shared decision-making with oncology.

### Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) (ACOG) — `gyn-pcos`

**Diagnosis (Rotterdam)**
- Rotterdam: need 2 of 3 criteria; PCOS is a diagnosis of exclusion
- Oligo- or anovulation: cycles >35 days or <9 menses/year
- Hyperandrogenism: clinical (hirsutism, acne) or biochemical (high free T)
- PCO morphology: ≥12 follicles 2–9 mm or ovarian volume >10 mL
**Exclude mimics**
- TSH (thyroid disease) and prolactin (prolactinoma) in all
- 17-OHP to rule out nonclassic CAH (21-hydroxylase deficiency)
- Rapid virilization: total testosterone + DHEAS for ovarian/adrenal tumor
**Management by goal**
- Cycle control: combined OCPs first-line (also improve hirsutism/acne)
- Fertility: letrozole first-line (higher live-birth rate than clomiphene)
- Metabolic: weight loss 5–10% + metformin for insulin resistance
- Hirsutism: OCP first; add spironolactone if inadequate after 6 months
**Endometrial protection & risks**
- Anovulation → unopposed estrogen → endometrial hyperplasia/cancer
- Protect endometrium: OCPs, cyclic progestin, or levonorgestrel IUD
- Screen: 75-g 2-hr OGTT (preferred over HbA1c), lipids, BP, mood
- Risks: T2DM, metabolic syndrome, OSA, NAFLD, depression/anxiety

*Pearls:* Ultrasound is unnecessary if oligo-anovulation and hyperandrogenism are both present, and PCO morphology should not be used to diagnose adolescents within 8 years of menarche. Classic trap: metformin is NOT first-line for ovulation induction — letrozole (off-label) beats clomiphene, especially at BMI >30. The 2018/2023 international guideline raises the follicle threshold to ≥20 per ovary with modern high-frequency transducers.

### Pelvic inflammatory disease (CDC) — `gyn-pid`

**Diagnosis — minimum criteria**
- Any ONE of: cervical motion, uterine, or adnexal tenderness
- Applies to sexually active women at risk for STIs with pelvic pain
- Treat presumptively — low threshold; PID is a clinical diagnosis
**Outpatient regimen**
- Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM x1 (1 g if weight ≥150 kg)
- Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID x 14 days
- Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID x 14 days
- Reassess in 72 h; admit if no clinical improvement
**Hospitalize (IV abx) if**
- Tubo-ovarian abscess
- Pregnancy
- Severe illness: high fever, N/V, cannot tolerate oral meds
- No response to or cannot take outpatient oral therapy
- Surgical emergency (e.g., appendicitis) not excluded
**Sequelae & IUD**
- Sequelae: tubal-factor infertility, ectopic pregnancy, chronic pelvic pain
- IUD stays in; remove only if no improvement in 48–72 h
- Treat partners from last 60 days; no sex until both complete therapy

*Pearls:* The 2021 CDC update raised ceftriaxone to 500 mg (from 250) and made metronidazole a standard part of the outpatient regimen for anaerobic coverage — older regimens omitted it. Negative gonorrhea/chlamydia NAATs do NOT rule out PID. Classic trap: the IUD comes out only for failure to improve after 48–72 h of antibiotics, never reflexively at diagnosis.

### Postmenopausal bleeding (ACOG) — `gyn-pmb`

**Workup & triage**
- PMB is endometrial cancer until proven otherwise (~10% harbor cancer)
- TVUS or endometrial biopsy (EMB): both acceptable first-line tests
- Stripe ≤4 mm: cancer risk <1% — biopsy not needed
- Stripe >4 mm, not visualized, or insufficient EMB → tissue (hysteroscopy/D&C)
- Persistent or recurrent bleeding → biopsy regardless of stripe
**Hyperplasia management**
- No atypia: progestin — 52-mg LNG-IUD preferred; <5% progress to cancer
- Atypia (EIN): total hysterectomy — ~40% harbor concurrent carcinoma
- Fertility/poor surgical candidate: high-dose progestin + EMB q3–6 mo
**Risk factors**
- Unopposed estrogen: estrogen-only HT with a uterus; chronic anovulation/PCOS
- Tamoxifen: work up any bleeding; no routine screening if asymptomatic
- Obesity (peripheral aromatization), diabetes, nulliparity, late menopause
- Lynch syndrome: 40–60% lifetime endometrial cancer risk

*Pearls:* Atrophy causes ~60% of PMB but is a diagnosis of exclusion — rule out cancer first. The ≤4-mm rule applies only to women who are bleeding: an incidentally thickened stripe in an asymptomatic woman is not an automatic biopsy (ACOG CO 734). Trap: blind Pipelle sampling can miss focal lesions, so bleeding that persists after a negative workup goes to hysteroscopy with D&C.

### Gonorrhea, chlamydia & syphilis treatment (CDC) — `gyn-sti`

**Gonorrhea**
- Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM x1; 1 g IM if weight ≥150 kg
- If chlamydia not excluded: add doxycycline 100 mg BID x 7 days
**Chlamydia**
- Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID x 7 days (first line)
- Pregnancy: azithromycin 1 g PO x1 (doxy contraindicated)
- Pregnancy: test of cure ~4 weeks after treatment
**Syphilis**
- Early (1°, 2°, early latent): benzathine PCN G 2.4 MU IM x1
- Late latent or unknown duration: benzathine PCN G 2.4 MU IM weekly x3
- Pregnant + PCN-allergic: desensitize and give PCN — no alternative
**Partners & follow-up**
- Treat partners from the past 60 days; EPT allowed for GC/CT
- Abstain from sex 7 days after treatment and until partners treated
- Retest GC/CT at 3 months — screens for reinfection, not test of cure

*Pearls:* The 2021 CDC update dropped dual therapy: ceftriaxone alone (500 mg, up from 250 mg) now covers gonorrhea, and doxycycline replaced azithromycin as chlamydia first line for better rectal/pharyngeal cure rates. In pregnant syphilis, a weekly injection missed by >9 days means restarting the whole series, and warn about Jarisch-Herxheimer — fever and contractions in the 24 hours after penicillin can precipitate preterm labor.

### Vaginitis (CDC) — `gyn-vaginitis`

**Bacterial vaginosis (BV)**
- Diagnose with ≥3 of 4 Amsel criteria
- Amsel: pH >4.5, clue cells, positive whiff, thin gray discharge
- Clue cells = epithelial cells stippled with bacteria
- Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID ×7 days (first-line)
- No partner treatment (not an STI)
**Candida (vulvovaginal)**
- Normal vaginal pH (3.8–4.5)
- Pseudohyphae/budding yeast on 10% KOH prep
- Thick white “cottage-cheese” discharge, no odor
- Fluconazole 150 mg PO ×1 (uncomplicated)
**Trichomoniasis**
- Motile flagellated protozoa on saline wet mount
- pH >4.5; strawberry cervix (colpitis macularis)
- Metronidazole 500 mg PO BID ×7 days (women)
- Treat all sex partners — it is an STI
**Diagnosis**
- Wet mount (saline + KOH) microscopy is first-line

*Pearls:* 2021 CDC changed trichomoniasis therapy in women to metronidazole 500 mg BID ×7 days; the single 2 g dose is now reserved for men. Both BV and trich raise pH >4.5, so pH separates candida (normal) from the other two — use clue cells vs. motile trichomonads to tell BV from trich. Wet mount misses ~50% of trich (NAAT is most sensitive), and metronidazole treats both BV and trich, but only trich requires partner treatment.
